Cheapest Protein Sources Ranked

We calculated the exact cost per gram of protein for 35 common foods using 2026 retail prices to find the most cost-effective ways to hit your macro targets.

Methodology

This 2026 study analyzed 35 of the most common protein sources consumed by fitness and health-conscious individuals. We aimed to definitively answer the question: What is the absolute cheapest source of protein?

Data collection followed these parameters:

  • Nutrition Data: Sourced from the USDA FoodData Central database to ensure accurate macronutrient profiles.
  • Pricing Data: Averaged from public retail prices across major US grocers (Walmart, Instacart) representing realistic, non-sale consumer pricing for standard quality goods.
  • Metric: "Protein Per Dollar" is calculated by dividing the total grams of protein in a serving by the average retail cost of that serving. Higher numbers mean better value.

Note: Prices vary by region, store, and season. Bulk purchases typically yield better protein-per-dollar ratios than listed here.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the cheapest source of protein?

According to our 2026 data, dry legumes like lentils and black beans are consistently the cheapest sources of protein, yielding over 50 grams of protein per dollar. Among animal sources, whole chickens and milk are typically the most cost-effective.

How much protein is in an egg?

One large egg (50g) contains approximately 6 grams of protein. While eggs are highly bioavailable, their rising retail prices have pushed them down the cost-efficiency rankings compared to plant-based options or even whey powder.

Is chicken breast the best protein source?

Chicken breast is exceptional for its high protein-to-calorie ratio (lean protein). It ranks highly for cost-effectiveness (around 35g of protein per dollar) and is one of the cheapest lean meat options available, making it a staple for bodybuilding and weight loss.

Are protein powders worth the money?

Yes. Despite the high upfront cost of a tub, whey protein powder is highly cost-effective per serving, typically yielding about 25 grams of protein per dollar. This makes it significantly cheaper than many whole-food sources like beef or fish.

What is the cheapest plant-based protein?

Dry lentils, dry black beans, and oats are the cheapest plant-based protein sources. While their protein is less dense per calorie than meat, they are incredibly cheap, offering upwards of 60 grams of protein per dollar spent.
